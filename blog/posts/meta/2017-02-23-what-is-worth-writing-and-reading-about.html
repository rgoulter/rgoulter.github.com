What is Worth Writing and Reading About

Posted on February 23, 2017 by Richard Goulter
Tags: writing, reading

Some time ago, I came across a blogpost discussing how to write a finite-domain constraint-propagation library to write a sudoku solver.
In a missing-the-point-of-doing-them kindof way, the author compared time spent playing sudoku as a denial-of-service attack on programmers’ minds.

I don’t think it’s useful to restrict actions to things which better society (or themselves). Nor that the author meant it that way. But perhaps people would be dicks to each other less often if we stopped to ask “is doing whatever going to make the world a better place, or a worse one?”.

On Twitter in particular, it’s common to come across accounts where the purpose-of-the-account is to pick out dumb shit others say, and lambaste others for saying dumb shit. – This has more value in feeling good about not being that person, although little persuasion value. I think the other hope, in doing this kind of thing, is to establish ‘ways to not think’ or patterns of incorrectness.

(Whether engineers at Twitter sit up at night thinking “does my work help make the world a better place, or worse?”, I don’t know).

I think my own recent -blogposts- rants are similar in this vein: frustration that others could be so hypocritical or righteous, without acknowledging (or qualifying) their opinions.
– It’s a wish that people would behave so as to not ‘be wrong’ in obnoxious ways.

I might describe my previous blogpost as “here’s something someone on the internet said; there’s some merit to it, but here’s what they’re wrong about”.
This isn’t always completely useless. And in terms of “I’ll write whatever I feel like writing”, cool.
But in terms of whether it benefits myself to write such things, or anyone else to read such things.. I suspect it’s not beneficial.

Pretty much anyone saying anything will be wrong about some things.
– Thus my hitherto disgruntlement of unqualified propagation of information and opinion. If opinions given are faulty, then it’s valuable to consider both its faults and its merits.
– But, rather, it prob’ly be more valuable to try to compose and synthesize new information from various other (faulty) blogposts and opinions, instead of wrapping a piece with “well, consider it’s wrong about this…”.

– That’s prob’ly more fun to write, although does require comparing multiple opinions.
The ideal is to have at-least two competing opinions, each doing a really good job of explaining the same phenomenon.
– But, as I’ve also wondered for some time, part of the limitation in exploring opinion-space (especially of unfamiliar domains) is how to representative (or accurate) pieces are.
