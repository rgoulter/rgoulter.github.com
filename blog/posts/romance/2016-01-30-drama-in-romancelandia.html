Drama in Romancelandia: On the Courtney Milan post about Bobbi Dumas

Posted on January 30, 2016 by Richard Goulter
Tags: argument, politics, diversity

One doesn’t tend to think of romancelandia as discordant.
Perhaps I’ve not been reading romances long enough. You’d also think that a genre set on characters having Happily Ever Afters, where characters get along after conflicting disagreeing with each other (& where the problem is almost always “poor communication”), wouldn’t have a problem with authors getting along with each other.

Despite this, here we see a woman (who is very sympathetic to the cause) being bullied because of an infraction.
Surprise, surprise.
This being the “incriminating” post in question, by the way.
– Dumas probably isn’t the first person thrown under the bus ‘for the greater good’. And she certainly won’t be the last in the romance community.

And, yes, this is very much “bullying”. Or perhaps “harassment”. Take your pick.
Milan isn’t ashamed to admit that she’s not being ‘nice’, because of course she’s being “good”. And presumably, then, the person she’s bullying deserves it. (I wonder if anyone in history bullied another without thinking it deserved?).

But my point isn’t that Milan is bullying a “bad” person. (I think Milan feels quite justified in doing that, it’s very much the activist frame of mind). But that she’s clearly bullying someone who is sympathetic to the cause. – It’s just too bad Dumas’ efforts weren’t enough, that despite being completely in favour of diverse romance (and listing several recommendations of diverse romance, on quite a well-known platform; in addition to other proactive efforts), she wasn’t pure enough. – And such a “with us or against us” attitude is surely concerning. (And considering how damn with them the target is!).
– Aside from the one quote (which she doesn’t link to), Milan never lists actual evidence that Dumas is guilty of a crime; that all the “good people” don’t like her. Just rumours, whispers. And we all believe rumours/whispers, right?

(By the way. If you’re somehow unaware of who Courtney Milan is, my standing recollection of her is “don’t misgender my dog”, which probably indicates where she stands politically. And while I’m unaware of what Dumas’ infractions are, I did see this encounter between Suleikha Snyder (who came up in my last post about romancelandia drama) and her, where Dumas is lambasted for using the phrase “namaste” while having the audacity to be born white. So.).

– If Milan’s treatment of someone on her side is so angry, her ranting can hardly surprise:

And! Not only that! The post then goes on to explain that the author’s GOAL is to reach those people [who don’t read diverse romance]. The racists.

Um, hell no.

Yes. Bobbi is reaching out to people who don’t read diverse romance, recommending diverse romances for them. – Maybe I’m a little dim, in that I don’t see the crime there.

Anyway, as to the trend of the socially-conscious bullying other socially-conscious women, one could say such “friendly fire” is a trend in domains other than romancelandia. I think, though, that’d be dragging a heavy baggage of politics into it, so it’s probably best to not go there.

One could also draw comparisons to other recent controversies about diversity and representation in entertainment; each side arguing to the other that they’re the ones who care about “diversity”, and LGBTQ+ people. I think there’d be even more baggage to that, though, so. – I suspect romancelandia isn’t going to get to such an intense divide, though.

The RWA so delicately described the above issue:

Romance Writers of America’s Board of Directors is aware of a difference in opinion regarding a column from a well-known reviewer on the subject of diversity and members of RWA who did not agree with the reviewer’s treatment of the subject.

Apparently a prominent author bullying women online isn’t a violation of the RWA code of ethics, so it’s not RWA’s job to pick a side when the kids fight on the playground.
Fair enough, then.

– Aside from the trend in general, regarding Dumas, and Milan’s post specifically:
Even if Dumas weren’t in favour of diversity, so what? There seems to be this nice thought that what’s “fair” and “equal” should win. (Which is where you get posts like this lamenting lack of anything not-straight, not-white. (Lack of “not-male” in publishing doesn’t concern the “it’s about equality” crowd, but anyway)). I dunno. If it’s true that more readers are black women, and people love buying books which fit their identity, then it makes sense that publishers (big or indie - we have the internet these days, don’t we?) stand to make more money by catering to a hitherto under-supplied market. And those who don’t want the money are free to not have it, I suppose.
– And if the market leads to the injustice that “what’s popular” doesn’t align with “what’s good or well-written” or “what’s diverse”, what use is it bullying some reviewer about it? (For fun? What’s it going to accomplish, aside from sowing bitterness?).

Yet, aside from such drama.. it’s disappointing to see romancelandia split like this. You’d think those of the genre-of-great-characters could do better. More constructively or collaboratively.
